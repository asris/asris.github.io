About

Bio

I am interested in understanding how molecular and cellular dysfunction disrupts neural circuits and drives neurological disease. My research focuses on uncovering the mechanisms that link protein homeostasis, epigenetic regulation, neuroinflammation, and other cellular pathologies to altered network function across neurodevelopmental, neurodegenerative, and epileptic disorders. Ultimately, I aim to translate these mechanistic insights into innovative strategies for neural repair and therapeutic intervention.

My scientific training began in neurodegeneration, where I investigated the molecular basis of Parkinson’s disease and Alzheimer’s disease through studies of protein aggregation, epigenetic regulation, neuroinflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and proteostasis failure. Those research focused on the interplay between BMI-1/PRC2–mediated epigenetic regulation, α-synuclein turnover, proteasomal degradation, and inflammatory signaling, providing insight into mechanisms that contribute to neurodegenerative pathology.

Building on this foundation, my current postdoctoral research at Children’s National Research Institute has expanded into epilepsy and neurodevelopmental disorders. I investigate how disruptions in brain development, inhibitory circuit formation, and long-range neuronal connectivity contribute to network hyperexcitability and epileptogenesis. My work spans a range of neurological conditions, including Dravet syndrome, Tuberous Sclerosis Complex, Focal Cortical Dysplasia, Down syndrome, fetal alcohol spectrum disorders and pediatric brain tumors. Across these disorders, I am particularly interested in identifying convergent mechanisms that connect molecular dysfunction to abnormal circuit activity and disease progression.

A major focus of my current research is the development of cell-based and bioengineered therapeutic approaches for neurological disease. This includes the co-development of the Biocage, an implantable neural delivery platform designed to enable localized and sustained delivery of cells or therapeutics to the brain. Through this work, I aim to bridge fundamental disease mechanisms with innovative translational strategies that can restore neural function and promote brain repair.

Across all of my research, I am driven by a central goal: to understand how molecular and cellular abnormalities reshape neural circuits and to use that knowledge to develop therapies that are both mechanistically informed and clinically meaningful. I am particularly drawn to interdisciplinary and collaborative science that integrates neuroscience, neuroengineering, stem cell biology, and translational medicine to address unmet challenges in neurological disease.
