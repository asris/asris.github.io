## Mentoring philosophy

I approach mentoring the same way I approach experimental design: with a bias toward reproducibility, rigorous documentation, and traceable causality. A well-mentored trainee should finish a project knowing not only what worked, but *why* — including which alternative hypotheses were ruled out along the way. In practice that means teaching assay design end-to-end (from experimental question through statistical analysis), enforcing electronic-lab-notebook discipline from day one, and asking trainees to interpret their own controls before I offer mine.

## Current mentoring

At the **Center for Neuroscience Research, Children's National Research Institute**, I mentor trainees across three overlapping technical domains that map onto our active research directions:

- **Assay design and execution** — quantitative immunoassays (ELISA, MSD, JESS/Western), flow cytometry, RT-qPCR, mitochondrial and proteostasis readouts.
- **Quality control and data integrity** — sample handling, replicate structure, blinding where feasible, and ELN discipline.
- **Quantitative data interpretation** — appropriate statistical framing (ANOVA and post-hoc structure, dose–response modelling, PK/PD-aligned sampling), figure design, and honest scoping of what a given experiment can and cannot conclude.

## Prior mentoring

During my doctoral work at the **Institute of Nano Science and Technology (INST), Mohali**, I supported junior graduate students on projects spanning α-synuclein proteostasis, amyloid-β aggregation, and epigenetically active nanotherapeutics — the same publication threads that now inform my current biomarker work.

## Formal teaching

I do not currently hold a formal course-teaching appointment. Trainees or students interested in short workshops on quantitative immunoassay design, HD-MEA data analysis, or biomarker workflow reproducibility are welcome to reach out.
